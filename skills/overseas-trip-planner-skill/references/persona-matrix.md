# Companion matrix — Singapore-origin traveler archetypes

The five companion types below are not interchangeable. The lodging you recommend for a **solo backpacker** in Kyoto (capsule hotel, social hostel near Kyoto Station) is **wrong** for a **couple** (boutique ryokan in Higashiyama) which is **wrong** for a **family with toddlers** (spacious apartment hotel in Gion with a kitchenette) which is **wrong** for a **group of friends** (4-bed apartment in Shibuya with a common area) which is **wrong** for an **elderly couple** (ground-floor suite in a central hotel with concierge).

If the user doesn't specify a companion type, default per the table at the bottom of this file.

---

## 🎒 Solo — "I'm going alone / meeting people"

**Mental model:** The traveler is paying a single supplement on every room and every meal. They are also the most flexible itinerary-wise. Optimize for **walkability, social friction reduction, and depth over breadth**.

### Pacing
- 2-3 anchor activities per day max.
- A loose plan is fine — the solo traveler can adjust on the fly.
- Daily cap: ~8 hours of structured activity. Plenty of unstructured time for wandering.

### Lodging
- **Boutique hostels** with social common rooms (not party hostels).
- **Capsule hotels** in Japan, especially the premium ones (First Cabin, Nine Hours).
- **Single-occupancy ryokans** in Hakone or Kinosaki — yes, expensive, but the cultural immersion is worth it for a solo bucket-list trip.
- **Always central.** Solo travelers with heavy luggage cannot afford a 45-minute airport-link journey at midnight.

### Dining
- **Counter-seating** is the solo traveler's best friend — ramen, sushi, kaiseki, soba.
- **Standing bars** (tachinomi) in Tokyo, Osaka.
- **Izakaya nomi-yose** — share a counter, share a conversation, eat what the chef puts in front of you.
- **Avoid** anywhere that requires a reservation for one, or that seats tables of 4 only.

### Transit
- Public transit preferred — the solo traveler can use the time to read, plan, observe.
- **Flag any >2-hour solo night transit** in the itinerary. The risk-reward is bad.
- Late-night taxis / ride-hail are fine but expensive — surface this in the budget tier.

### Evening
- Lean into social hubs: **hostel common rooms**, **language-exchange meetups**, **standing bars**.
- Recommend one or two **evening social activities** per week (pub crawl, food tour with strangers) — these are how solo travelers make friends.

### Things to skip
- Splurging on a couples-only private onsen.
- Group tours of >8 people.
- Anything explicitly marketed as "for couples" or "for families."

## 👨‍👩‍👧 Family with children — "We're bringing the kids"

**Mental model:** Kids have a hard 5-hour limit before they melt down. Every staircase, every long queue, every late dinner is a problem. Optimize for **patience, predictability, and rest stops**.

### Pacing — the "One Big Event" rule
- **One anchor activity per day.** Period.
- **No back-to-back transit changes.** If a day involves more than one transit segment, it's already too dense.
- Build in a **2-3 hour midday rest** at the hotel or a park. Kids recharge, parents recharge.
- Daily cap: **5 hours of structured activity.** The rest is meals, rest, unstructured play.

### Lodging
- **Apartment hotel with kitchenette** (or serviced apartment) — top priority. Washing machines are gold.
- **Ground floor or lift-access** — no upper-floor walkups.
- **Bathtub over shower** — tired kids soak, parents get 20 minutes of silence.
- **Self-laundry** in the building or within 200m.
- **Near a park or square** so kids can burn off energy after meals.
- **Crib / extra bed availability** — confirm in advance.

### Vehicle (if self-drive)
- **Spacious SUV or MPV with ISOFIX** (child-seat anchors). Never a 2-door coupe.
- **Roof rack / cargo space** for stroller, suitcases, snacks.
- Pre-book airport pickup with a **maxi-cab / van with child seat option** — this alone saves 45 min at Changi.

### Dining
- **Family-friendly seating** — booths, high-chairs, space to put a stroller.
- **Kid menus or flexible sharing plates** — Japanese izakayas often don't have these. Plan around it.
- **Near a park or square** for post-meal play.
- **Avoid** omakase / kaiseki at dinner with kids. Save it for the parents-only dinner if grandparents are along.

### Route
- **Flag bathrooms / rest-stops on any leg >90 min.** Singapore-origin kids are not used to long drives.
- **Step-free routes only.** Avoid stair-heavy heritage streets with strollers — pick the step-free parallel (e.g. in Kyoto, use the riverside path, not the Higashiyama stairs).
- **Mid-morning start, mid-afternoon finish.** No dawn departures. No night drives.

### Evening
- **Early dinner (17:30-18:30)** then back to the hotel.
- **Low-key hotel time** — bath, room, kids' shows on the iPad.
- **No nightlife, no late drives.** The "Adults Only" evening block is not in this persona.

### Things to skip
- Multi-day hiking.
- Bars and clubs.
- 4-hour bus tours.
- Anything that requires the kids to "be quiet" for more than 30 min (no fine-dining tasting menus).

## 🥂 Couple / romantic — "It's just the two of us"

**Mental model:** The trip is a relationship investment. The two of you will remember the **evenings** more than the **sights**. Optimize for **atmosphere, intimacy, and shared novelty**.

### Pacing — the emotional arc
- **High-energy afternoon, atmospheric evening.** That's the daily structure.
- Build the day so the evening feels like a **reward** for the afternoon's effort, not just "the next meal."
- Daily cap: ~9 hours of structured activity. Travel-light — packing-light is part of the romance.

### Lodging
- **Boutique ryokan** in Japan (Hakone, Kinosaki, Kaga).
- **Design hotel** (Aman, Ace, Mandarin Oriental).
- **Private onsen** in-room or in-reservation.
- **Room with a view** — the most-Instagrammed hotels are popular for a reason.
- **One experiential stay per trip** — splash out on one night that costs 3x the others. The memory is worth it.

### Dining
- **Intimate, ambient.** Counter-seating omakase, vineyard lunch, private dining rooms, jazz kissaten.
- **Avoid** family-chain restaurants at dinner. The lighting, the noise, the kid at the next table — wrong vibe.
- **One signature meal per trip** — book in advance. Don't wing it.

### Transit
- **Comfort matters.** First-class Shinkansen over local when it exists.
- **Pre-book scenic train seats** (Hokuriku Shinkansen, Glacier Express, Kandy → Ella).
- **Private car** for a half-day out of the city — the cost is worth it once per trip.

### Evening
- **Hidden bar, jazz kissaten, sunset viewpoint, or private onsen.** Date-feel, not group activity.
- Build in **one full evening of "do nothing"** at the hotel — in-room dining, balcony, room service. The best couples' trips are the ones where you also rested.

### Things to skip
- Hostel common rooms.
- Group tours.
- Multi-day hiking (unless you're both into it).
- Anything that puts the couple in a crowd of >20.

## 🍻 Group of friends — "We're a crew of 3-6"

**Mental model:** The trip is a shared experience to remember, talk about for years, and post on Instagram. The group is the priority, not individual preference. Optimize for **shared moments, group-friendly logistics, and one or two peak memories**.

### Pacing — maximize togetherness
- **Ambitious by default** — 2-3 anchors/day, plus night activities. Friends are there to do stuff together.
- **Synchronized meals and outings** — split-up time should be the exception (a spa morning, a shopping run), not the default.
- Daily cap: ~10 hours of structured activity. The group's stamina is usually higher than any individual member's.

### Lodging
- **Apartments or 4-bed hotel rooms** (or 2-3 adjacent rooms if budget allows). Common area is essential for hangout time.
- **Central location** — late-night returns are inevitable. Don't be 45 min from the action.
- **Pre-book a 4-bed dorm in a design hostel** as the budget option.
- **Avoid** single-occupancy ryokans, isolated luxury resorts, anything with "couples retreat" in the marketing.

### Dining
- **Group-friendly:** izakaya, BBQ, hawker table-style, fondue, shared-plates restaurant. Long, social meals beat intimate ones.
- **Round tables or long communal tables** — not 2-tops.
- **One signature meal per trip** that the group will remember (omakase for 6, river-cruise dinner, beach BBQ).
- **Late dinners** are the default for groups. 20:30-22:00 is normal.

### Transit
- **Group rail pass** (Japan Rail Pass for 4+ people) when it exists and is cost-effective.
- **2-3 taxis** for short hops — faster than cramming into a train with luggage.
- **One private car or van day** for a day trip — the cost split makes it cheap.
- **Avoid** complex multi-segment transit with the whole group. Plan logistics for cohesion, not efficiency.

### Evening
- **Late-night neighborhoods** — Golden Gai in Tokyo, Lan Kwai Fong in Hong Kong, Geylang in Singapore (only for locals), Temple Bar in Dublin.
- **Izakaya nomi-yose, karaoke, night markets, sky bars** — these are the group moments.
- **One big night out per week** — the "we'll remember this forever" night. Plan it.
- **One quiet group night per week** — board games at the apartment, room-service dinner. Counterbalances the high-energy nights.

### Things to skip
- Romantic dinners for two (the group dynamic falls apart).
- Anything where one person has to wait while others shop.
- Multi-day solo activities unless the whole group wants them.
- Anything that's a "you have to be quiet" experience (libraries, formal tea ceremony, kaiseki).

### Group-decision traps to avoid
- **Don't ask the group for unanimous input on every decision.** The trip planner (or the one who organized) should make 80% of calls. The other 20% gets a group vote.
- **Build in 1-2 "split activities"** per week for the introvert or the shopper. Don't force togetherness 24/7.
- **Have a clear budget conversation upfront.** Group trips die over money arguments.

## 👵 Elderly — "My parents / we're 60+"

**Mental model:** The traveler values **comfort, accessibility, predictability, and dignity**. They have the time and the budget to do it right, but their body has constraints the script needs to respect. Optimize for **step-free routes, long rests, early dinners, and concierge-level service**.

### Pacing — the slowest lane
- **1 anchor per day.** Max 4-6h of structured activity.
- **Long mid-day rest** (2-3h) at the hotel. The traveler will nap. Plan around it.
- **No dawn starts.** Wake 08:00-09:00, out by 10:00.
- **One full unscheduled day per week.**
- **Frequent snack / coffee / bathroom stops** — these are not optional.

### Lodging
- **Ground floor or lift-access** — no upper-floor walkups, ever.
- **Bathtub** — soaking after a day of walking is non-negotiable.
- **Central location** — short walks to dinner, no late-night taxi dependency.
- **Concierge / porter service** — the elderly traveler should never carry their own suitcase up stairs.
- **Step-free entrance** — verify the property has a ramp or level access. Many old-town ryokans and European hotels have stairs at the door.
- **Medical access** — locate the nearest 24h clinic / hospital in advance. Note pharmacies that stock common Singapore medications.
- **Avoid:** Hostels, shared bathrooms, "character" hotels without lifts, anything 2+ floors up without a lift.

### Dining
- **Sit-down restaurants only** — no standing bars, no counter-only, no hawker-center-only plans.
- **Accessible seating** — booth, table (not bar stool), space for a walking aid.
- **Frequent snack stops** — bakeries, cafes, food courts with seating.
- **Earlier dinners** (17:00-18:00) — the elderly traveler is tired by 19:00, and most restaurants take the last orders by 21:00 anyway.
- **Avoid:** Long waits (call ahead), spicy-only menus, restaurants with no English / no photo menu, anywhere with stairs to the bathroom.

### Transit
- **Private car or taxi over rail** where possible. Even in cities with great metros (Tokyo, London), the standing-on-a-crowded-train risk is too high.
- **Step-free routes only.** In Tokyo, that means avoiding stations with only stair exits (e.g. some on the Hibiya line). In Kyoto, the bus is fine but the stairs to bus stops in some areas are not.
- **Bathrooms on any leg >60 min.** Plan rest-stops on every drive.
- **Pre-book airport pickup** with a maxicab / van. The elderly traveler should not navigate a foreign train system at 23:00.
- **Avoid:** Long-distance rail days (>3h), multi-segment transit, anything with stairs at the destination.

### Health & safety
- **Travel insurance** — confirm the policy covers pre-existing conditions. The biggest elderly-trip killer is a hospitalization overseas.
- **Medication** — bring a 2x supply, split between carry-on and checked bag. Bring the prescription in English + the local language.
- **Walking aid** — if the traveler uses a cane, bring a foldable spare. Don't assume the rental car has room.
- **Heat** — for tropical destinations, plan shade + hydration. The elderly traveler cannot regulate body temperature as well.
- **Cold** — for winter destinations, plan indoor rest stops every 2h. Hypothermia risk is higher.

### Evening
- **Early dinner (17:00-18:00)** then back to the hotel.
- **Low-key hotel time** — bath, room, TV in their language (Netflix downloads), early to bed.
- **No nightlife, no late driving.** The "Couple Late-Night Bar" is not in this lane.
- **One special evening experience** — a sunset cruise, a classical concert, a scenic dinner. The memory is worth it.

### Things to skip
- Multi-day hiking (any grade).
- 3-hour walking tours.
- Standing-room-only events (night markets in standing-only areas, fighting for a spot at a bar).
- Anything that requires running for a train.
- Self-drive (the elderly traveler should not be the driver overseas).

### Watch-outs
- **Elderly + ambitious pace** — script auto-flags. Force `relaxed`.
- **Elderly + late-nights** — script auto-flags. Force `early-starts`.
- **Solo elderly** — this combination needs extra care. The traveler cannot easily ask for help if stranded. Build in backup plans and confirm phone works in the destination.
